## Overview

**Topic:** How AI is fundamentally shifting the engineer's role from writing code to orchestrating outcomes — and why the engineers who thrive are treating themselves as the PM of a small agent team.

**Angle/Hook:** There are two types of engineers navigating the AI moment right now. Type A is optimizing typing. Type B is optimizing outcomes. The gap between them is growing fast — and it's not about which tools you use, it's about which paradigm you're operating in.

**Target Audience:** Senior engineers and engineering managers who are actively using AI tools and want to think more clearly about how to use them well. Also valuable for engineering directors thinking about how the role of their teams is changing.

**Word Counts:**
- Blog Post: 1200-1500 words
- Newsletter: 150-200 words

## Key Message

The engineers who win with AI aren't the ones who use it most — they're the ones who shifted their mental model from "I write code" to "I orchestrate outcomes." That shift requires thinking less like a craftsperson and more like a product manager running a team of fast, tireless agents.

---

## Story/Hook

**Setting:** An engineering team's Slack channel or standup — a place where most engineers are adopting AI tools but following very different playbooks. An overheard conversation becomes the perfect lens.

**Challenge:** An engineer describes how they use their AI coding assistant — chatting with it during coding sessions, sometimes accepting its suggestions. On the surface, this looks like AI adoption. But there's a problem: this engineer is still fundamentally a typist. They've added a smarter autocomplete. The paradigm hasn't changed.

**Action:** Contrast that with the engineer who doesn't open their coding assistant to chat mid-function. Instead, they start their day with a JIRA ticket or PRD, break it down into a structured plan, dispatch that plan to an agentic workflow, review the resulting pull request, and push it through the team's review process — all before lunch.

**Result:** The first engineer saves some keystrokes. The second engineer ships full features. The output delta is not incremental — it's multiplicative.

**Learning:** The tool isn't the variable. The paradigm is. Type A asks the AI how to do something. Type B tells the AI what problem to solve. One is refining the old workflow. The other is operating in an entirely new one.

## Main Arguments / Key Points

### Point 1: The Two Types — Why Paradigm Matters More Than the Tool

- **Core idea:** Most engineers currently using AI are Type A: they tell the AI *how* to implement something and stay in the driver's seat of implementation. Type B engineers flip the relationship — they describe *what* problem needs solving and let the AI handle the how. This is not a minor distinction. It's a complete inversion of where human judgment lives in the workflow.
- **Evidence:** A 2025 study found that while 75% of engineers use AI tools, most organizations see no measurable gains in delivery at the company level. This is exactly what you'd expect if the majority of engineers are Type A — adding AI to their existing workflow rather than adopting a new one. Meanwhile, teams where developers operate as orchestrators are seeing multiplicative, not incremental, gains.
- **Practical application:** Take your next task. Before opening any tool, write a one-paragraph description of the problem you need to solve — the *what*, not the *how*. Then give that to your AI system and see what comes back before you touch a single line of code. That's the starting posture of a Type B engineer.

### Point 2: The Engineer as PM — What the New Workflow Actually Looks Like

- **Core idea:** When you treat AI as a team member rather than an autocomplete, your job description quietly changes. You're no longer the implementer — you're the one defining requirements, reviewing output, iterating on quality, and pushing results through the team's review gates. That's a product manager's job, applied to an agent team.
- **Evidence:** McKinsey's research on agentic organizations found that "a human team of two to five people can already supervise an agent factory of 50 to 100 specialized agents running an end-to-end process." The leverage is real — but only if the humans running those agents are thinking at the right level of abstraction. The demand is rising specifically for engineers who can "define standards and orchestrate development work across internal and external teams, vendors, and agents."
- **Practical application:** Map your current sprint tasks into two columns: things you are writing yourself, and things you could hand off to an agent with a well-written prompt or structured input. Most engineers are surprised how much belongs in the second column. Start there. The PM mindset shows up in how you write the ticket, not how you write the code.

### Point 3: The Ownership Bar Shifts — Engineers Can Now Own More Scope

- **Core idea:** When the cost of implementation drops, the ceiling on what a single engineer can own rises. Ideas that used to require a two-sprint roadmap can now be prototyped in a day. This changes what it means for an engineer to take ownership of a problem — they can go further, faster, without waiting for team bandwidth to open up.
- **Evidence:** The Allstacks analysis of AI-driven development notes that engineers who adopt the orchestrator model take on "the micro product decisions that AI cannot make, filling gaps that AI cannot identify, and exercising product judgment that specs could never fully capture." The scope of ownership expands, but so does the type of judgment required.
- **Practical application:** The next time you're scoping a feature, don't start with "how long will this take to build?" Start with "what decision needs to be made here, and what do I need to know to make it?" The build is now a smaller fraction of the work. The judgment is the job.

### Point 4: The Leadership Shift — From Unblocking to Enabling Autonomy

- **Core idea:** For engineering directors and managers, the Type A vs Type B dynamic plays out at the team level. When engineers can move faster and own more scope, the manager's role shifts. You spend less time unblocking and more time setting guardrails. Less "let me clear the path" and more "go — but keep security, due diligence, and quality in frame."
- **Evidence:** The pattern shows up in reporting and operations too — AI now handles synthesis and formatting of DORA metrics, performance review inputs, hiring processes, and leadership presentations. That frees leaders to focus on the judgment calls that require context AI doesn't have: organizational dynamics, trust calibration, strategic sequencing.
- **Practical application:** In your next team planning session, identify one area where you're currently serving as the bottleneck — a decision that only flows through you. Ask whether the engineer on the other side of that bottleneck could own it with the right guardrails in place. If yes, write the guardrail document together and hand it off. That's what enabling autonomy looks like in practice.

## Framework/Model

**Name:** The Type A / Type B Paradigm Shift

**Components:**
1. **Type A — Typing Optimization:** Engineer uses AI to write code faster. Human stays in implementation seat. AI is a smarter autocomplete. Outcome: incremental gains.
2. **Type B — Outcome Orchestration:** Engineer uses AI to solve problems. Human sits in the planning, review, and judgment seat. AI is a fast, capable team member. Outcome: multiplicative gains.
3. **The PM Layer:** Type B engineers think like product managers. They write requirements, dispatch work, review outputs, maintain quality standards, and push results through review gates. The question they ask is not "how do I write this?" but "what needs to happen, and did it happen correctly?"
4. **The Leadership Corollary:** When engineers move to Type B, managers move from unblocking to enabling. Guardrails replace bottlenecks. Autonomy is the outcome.

**How it works:** The framework forms a progression. Individual engineers shift from Type A to Type B. As they do, their scope of ownership expands. As team scope expands, management structure shifts from control to enablement. The whole organization operates at a higher level of abstraction — which is exactly where AI can multiply human judgment rather than just replacing human effort.

## Practical Takeaways

1. **Audit your AI usage for the next 3 days.** Every time you use an AI tool, note whether you're telling it *how* to do something (Type A) or telling it *what* to solve (Type B). Most engineers are surprised by the ratio.

2. **Before writing a single line of code on your next ticket, write a one-paragraph problem statement.** Hand that to your AI system as the starting point. Don't touch the implementation until you've seen what comes back and decided how to evaluate it.

3. **Write your review criteria before reviewing AI output.** Define what "done" looks like before you look at the generated code or design. This forces you into the PM mindset — and it catches more issues than reviewing with fresh eyes.

4. **If you manage a team, identify one bottleneck you're currently holding.** Write a one-page guardrail document that gives the engineer on the other side of that bottleneck the context to make the call themselves. Hand it off and debrief in two weeks.

5. **Pick one workflow — a recurring report, a standup summary, a design prototype — and hand the entire thing to an agentic workflow this week.** The goal is not to save ten minutes. The goal is to experience what it feels like to orchestrate rather than execute, so you can start extending that model intentionally.

## Metrics/Measurement

- **Features shipped per sprint, before and after shifting to a Type B workflow.** Not PR volume (that's a Type A metric), but features that are reviewed, merged, and in production.
- **Time from ticket to PR, tracked over 4 weeks after adopting the orchestrator model.** For most engineers, this compresses dramatically when they stop treating AI as autocomplete and start treating it as a team member.
- **Reduction in manager bottlenecks, measured as decisions that route through the director vs. decisions made autonomously by the team.** Useful for leaders tracking the leadership corollary of the framework.

## Common Objections/Questions

**Objection:** "I still need to understand the code — I can't just trust what the AI generates."
**Response:** Correct. Type B engineers review everything. The shift is not from reviewing to blind trust. It's from writing to reviewing. A senior engineer who can evaluate a PR in 20 minutes is more valuable than one who spent 4 hours writing it. Your judgment about the output is the job — and that judgment gets sharper the more you practice it.

**Objection:** "This only works for simple tasks. Complex architectural work still requires a human in the seat."
**Response:** Yes and no. Architectural decisions still require deep human judgment. But the implementation of architectural decisions — the code, the scaffolding, the configuration — is increasingly something agents can handle with the right inputs. The question is: are you spending your time on the judgment calls, or on the scaffolding? Type B engineers protect their time for the former.

**Question:** "How do I convince my team to shift from Type A to Type B if they're resistant?"
**Answer:** Don't start with the concept. Start with a demo. Find one concrete workflow — sprint planning, ticket breakdown, PR generation from a JIRA ticket — and run it through an agentic workflow in a team session. Let people see the output. The paradigm shift is easier to feel than to argue.
